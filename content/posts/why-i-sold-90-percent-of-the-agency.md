# Why I Built an Agency, Sold 90% of It, and Started Over

The conventional wisdom about agency ownership is that you build it, you grow it, you either sell it to a larger holding company or you run it forever. You do not, under that conventional wisdom, sell most of the equity to your partner, keep a minority stake, and start a new firm from scratch.

In 2023 I did exactly that. I sold 90% of Kaleidico — the demand generation agency I founded in 2005 — to my partner [Ken Gemmell](https://kaleidico.com/) and started [Bill Rice Strategy Group](https://billricestrategy.com/) a few months later.

People have asked me about that decision more than any other career move I've made. Here's the honest version.

## What Kaleidico was

Kaleidico is the B2C lead generation agency I founded in 2005 after leaving Quicken Loans. At its core, Kaleidico does what most demand generation agencies claim to do but few actually execute: build sustainable lead generation systems for regulated industries — mortgage lenders, law firms, senior living communities. We ran content, SEO, PPC, lead nurturing, and sometimes call centers, end-to-end.

I built the original product — icoSales, an intelligent lead scoring and distribution platform — during that era. I coined the term "lead management" to describe what icoSales did (a punch line that stuck and is now an entire industry category). I built out the team, the methodology, and the playbooks.

By 2020, Kaleidico had a real book of business, an established reputation in mortgage and legal, and a capable operating team. It didn't need me sitting in the CEO seat every day.

That was the setup for the sale.

## The decision I was actually making

When people ask "why did you sell," they usually assume the answer is financial — you took a big check and walked away. That is not what happened.

The decision I was making was not "do I want money." It was "what do I want the next 20 years of my career to look like?"

Two things were true:

**First:** Running a full-service B2C demand generation agency is an operating job. Headcount, client delivery, payroll, vendor management, compliance. It is a very particular kind of work, and it consumes most of the oxygen in your week. I had done it for almost two decades and done it well. I did not want to do it for two more.

**Second:** I had become increasingly interested in the work that happens before the agency engagement — the strategic questions fintech and B2B companies were asking about GTM, positioning, content, lead programs, compliance. That work is fundamentally different from running an agency. It's advisory. It's in the C-suite. It's project-based, not retainer-based. It's where my 30+ years of experience actually earn a premium, because you're not competing on agency rates.

The choice was: run Kaleidico for another ten years as CEO, or structure an exit that let me keep equity exposure to the agency while freeing my time to build something new.

I chose the second.

## Why sell to Ken instead of selling to outside buyers

Outside buyers for agency equity are, primarily, holding companies and private equity roll-ups. I've seen how those deals go. The founder sells, signs a three-year earnout, watches the culture get systematically destroyed by corporate consolidation, and either leaves angry or stays miserable until the earnout clears.

The other path — selling to a strategic — has its own problems. The acquirer wants your book of business and your top people and will unwind the brand eventually.

What I wanted was for Kaleidico to keep being Kaleidico. Same team, same methodology, same clients getting served the same way. I also wanted to keep meaningful equity exposure because I genuinely believe the agency will be worth substantially more in ten years than it's worth today.

[Ken Gemmell](https://kaleidico.com/) was the obvious answer. Ken was already the tech and business partner at Kaleidico. He knew the operation, the team trusted him, and he had the appetite to run it as majority owner. Selling the controlling stake to him — 90% — gave him the autonomy he needed to run it his way, and kept me at 10% as a continuing minority owner and occasional strategic advisor.

Is 10% a smaller financial stake than 100%? Yes. Is it a larger expected financial value than the alternative? Almost certainly. Ken running Kaleidico full-time will grow the business faster than I could running it part-time while distracted by other ventures.

## The "starting over" part

A few months after the Kaleidico sale closed, I stood up Bill Rice Strategy Group.

BRSG is intentionally different from Kaleidico. Different target market (B2B fintech instead of B2C regulated industries), different service model (advisory and strategic content instead of full-funnel demand gen), different economics (project-based and retainer strategy work instead of performance-driven lead generation), different org structure (small senior team instead of a delivery org).

The first year of BRSG has been — honestly — fun in a way that the twentieth year of Kaleidico could never have been. New positioning. New offers. New client categories. The ability to say no to engagements that don't match the thesis. The ability to build a firm that matches what I know now, rather than what I knew in 2005.

I'm also writing more. [The Lead Brief](https://theleadbrief.com/) newsletter, [The Lead Buyer's Playbook](https://www.leadbuyerplaybook.com/), this blog, speaking engagements, podcasts. That surface area wasn't really compatible with being full-time CEO of an operating agency. It is compatible with running an advisory firm.

And — critically — I've been able to launch a third company, [Verified Vector](https://verifiedvector.com/), focused on programmatic SEO and AI marketing for fintech. That never happens if I'm still running Kaleidico.

## What I'd tell another founder thinking about this

If you're reading this as an agency owner somewhere in years 10-20 of your business, wondering whether you should keep running it or move on, here's what I've learned from making the move:

**Equity vs. income is the real decision.** The question isn't "should I sell." The question is "what mix of equity, income, and optionality do I want for the next decade." Most founders over-weight income and under-weight optionality. Selling a majority stake while keeping equity exposure is a way to trade income (CEO comp) for optionality (time to build something else).

**You can't build something new while running something old.** I thought for a long time that I could run Kaleidico at 40% attention and do advisory/writing/new ventures with the other 60%. That was never going to work. Operating businesses consume all the oxygen you give them. If you want to build something new, you have to free the hours.

**Pick your successor carefully and transfer authority completely.** Half-transfers don't work. If you sell 51% but stay involved in every decision, you've just made your partner miserable and kept the operating burden. The value of the deal is their autonomy.

**Stay on the cap table if you believe in the business.** Founders who fully exit often regret it if the business continues to grow. Keeping minority equity in a business you know well, run by a partner you trust, is usually a better risk-adjusted bet than deploying the cash elsewhere.

**Don't romanticize what you built.** Whatever you're running today is the product of a specific moment in your life and the market conditions at the time. If you're 20 years in, you know more now than the version of you who designed the firm. Starting over, informed by 20 years of learning, is a real edge.

## The agency is doing fine

This is the part I want to be clear about: Kaleidico is doing very well. Ken has grown the business since the sale. The team is stable. The client book is healthy. The brand is stronger than it's ever been. I talk to Ken regularly as a minority shareholder and sometimes-advisor, but I don't run anything day-to-day, and the agency does not need me to.

That's the outcome I was optimizing for. Kaleidico keeps being Kaleidico, under leadership that has more time and energy than I did. I get to build BRSG and Verified Vector on a clean slate, write the playbooks that have been sitting in my head for 30 years, and still maintain exposure to the agency I built.

It's not the conventional exit. But I think the conventional exit is wrong for most agency founders, and I wrote this to explain why.
